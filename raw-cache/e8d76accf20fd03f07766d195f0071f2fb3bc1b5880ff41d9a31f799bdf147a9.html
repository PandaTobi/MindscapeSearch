Welcome to the August 2025 Ask Me Anything episode of Mindscape! These monthly excursions are funded by Patreon supporters (who are also the ones asking the questions). We take questions asked by Patreons, whittle them down to a more manageable number -- based primarily on whether I have anything interesting to say about them, not whether the questions themselves are good -- and sometimes group them together if they are about a similar topic. Enjoy!

0:00:08.0 SC: Not a lot of news to report since the last AMA a month ago. The life in the summertime of a college professor trying to get papers written and books written, does not really create a lot of adventures to relate. But that's okay. Sometimes, the adventures are in the mind. That's important also. There was a fun article that came out in Nature. They tried to figure out what physicists really think about the foundations of quantum mechanics. This is something that happens every so often that we try to take a poll of a bunch of physicists, and ask them what they really think. The problem is with that kind of approach, that it's going to be very, very biased depending on who exactly you are polling. You're polling your friends, you're polling the people whose emails you happen to have, you're polling people at a certain kind of conference. Those sets of people might be very different in the kinds of quantum mechanics that they like.     I suspect the landscape of established interpretation is “Everettian” in the sense that as progress is made, we will not see a pruning of interpretations and convergence towards some unique correct interpretation, but rather a branching of equally viable, robust, mature interpretations. Asher Peres has done much to perfect Copenhagen/instrumentalism. Wallace/Carroll/Deutsch have done much to perfect Many Worlds. Omnes, Griffiths, Gell-Mann, and Hartle have done much to perfect consistent histories etc etc.

    Objective metrics are “How ambiguous is an interpretation?” “Is it generalizable to all quantum theories?” “Has it contributed insight to other research projects in physics?” and many interpretations meet these standards. More subjective metrics like “Is it realist?” “Is it determinist?” “Is it local?” “Is it Markovian?” might help map out preferences, but there’s little reason to think all physicists would have the same preferences.
