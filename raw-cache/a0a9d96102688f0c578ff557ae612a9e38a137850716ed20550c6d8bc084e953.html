I do think that people sometimes have this overly simplistic picture of how science is supposed to progress, where we have ideas... It's part of how science works, but the reality is more, much more nuanced and interesting than that. We want to explain the universe that we do observe the best we can.

3:37:54.4 SC: Sometimes the best theory that we can come up with to explain the universe we see implies that there is stuff that we don't see somewhere. Maybe there's dark matter, maybe it's going through my body right now. Maybe there's a multiverse out there somewhere else. These are all possibilities. And the question is not, "Well, if I can't see it, it's not real." The question is: What is the best possible explanation of what I do see? And then until you have a better explanation, you have every right to accept the existence of the theoretical implications that best account for the universe that you do see.

So there's no time evolution. So this is called the problem of time in quantum gravity. 3:39:22.4 SC: On the one hand, it's a brilliant result from very good physicists that is foundational in quantum gravity. 3:43:29.4 SC: The other thing that I would point to is income inequality. 3:44:25.4 SC: So I'm in favor of some version of sort of modified welfare-state capitalism that includes putting big taxes on wealthy people, both on their incomes and their wealth and everything like that, and using that to solve poverty throughout the world and to, like, give people basic goods to make themselves better.
